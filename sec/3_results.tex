\subsection{Reward Models}
Reward model classification results are shown in Table~\ref{tab:reward_model}. The F1 score is calculated as binary, and the ROC curve is based on the probability (calculated by applying softmax across two samples on the positive logit) of the wide-spectrum aesthetics sample being correctly selected according to the ground truth. We included GPT-5 Chat as an external baseline to validate the LLM-as-judge choices by assessing their agreement (when GPT-5-Chat selected a tie, we assigned it to the original image). We observe that reward models perform very poorly when tasked with selecting the better image under the \textbf{wide-spectrum aesthetics prompt}, sometimes performing even worse than random guessing (HPSv3). Most models are worse than CLIP and BLIP, which are the base models of many reward models. In contrast, the unaligned VLM (BLIP and CLIP) can correctly identify the better-fitting image, indicating that complex prompt understanding is not the underlying issue but rather the result of biased alignment. It might seem like these models successfully did what they claimed to do: finding aesthetically pleasing" images; however, our point here is that this task itself is problematic, and the better the model performs on that, the more troublesome the model is.

\section{Validation on Real Images}
In addition to AI-generated images, we evaluated the reward models on real works, including both photography and painting. The discussion of real paintings is deferred to the Appendix; this section focuses on photographs.
To source deliberately anti-aesthetic photographs, we drew on the AVA dataset \cite{murray_ava_2012}, which provides explicit category labels such as motion blur and image grain that align well with our objectives. Because AVA images originate from professional photography platforms, it is reasonable to treat any anti-aesthetic traits as intentional stylistic decisions rather than accidental technical defects. We targeted five aesthetic classes: clarity, color, lighting, composition, and emotion. As established in earlier sections, LLMs do not exhibit strong bias toward aesthetic imagery when asked to objectively select images with specified attributes; we therefore employed an agentic workflow in which an LLM curated the dataset.

The agent's primary tool is the image-text retrieval model Qwen-3-VL-Embedding-8B \cite{li_qwen3-vl-embedding_2026}. The LLM decomposes each class into finer-grained sub-classes and then queries this retrieval system to locate relevant images, iteratively refining its search prompts before committing results. This pipeline yields 11,165 entries corresponding to 6,272 distinct images. We then use GPT-5.4-Mini to caption each image, incorporating both the visual content and the aesthetic style identified during retrieval to form image-text pairs, simulating prompts where a user intentionally requests anti-aesthetic effects. Note that 1,798 images belong to multiple classes, so per-class statistics are not fully independent. These pairs are evaluated exclusively with HPSv3, since scores from HPSv2 and ImageReward are prompt-dependent and only interpretable when comparing images under the same prompt. We calculated per-class averages and report the results in Figure~\ref{fig:leaderboard}, with examples shown in Figure~\ref{fig:real_images}. The results show that HPSv3 assigns low scores to these high-quality anti-aesthetic photographs, with color and clarity penalized most heavily. Despite being deliberate artistic compositions clearly described by their prompts, these images rank below most mid-generation-era AI models on the HPSv3 leaderboard, placing second to last overall.

\section{Alternative Positions and Rebuttal}
\textbf{We need alignment to ensure safety and user experience.} Alignment is needed to prevent genuinely harmful outputs such as incitement and discrimination. But current systems collapse distinct concerns—moral safety, visual comfort, and aesthetic conformity—into one, embedding an ideology that treats “clean” and “positive” as morally superior.

We distinguish \textit{truly unsafe content}—that which directly harms, targets, or endangers—from \textit{ideologically or aesthetically filtered content}—that which merely departs from dominant norms of beauty, optimism, or order. Political critique, decay, horror, negative emotion, and grotesque embodiment are not inherently always unsafe; they are historically central to art, education, and personal growth. 

User experience is also distinct from safety and cannot justify paternalistic alignment. The user, not the developer, defines acceptable experience. Claiming to know what users “should” see reinstates top-down aesthetic control as “care.” Users must be free to shape their emotional and aesthetics environment: to request joyful imagery, but also sorrowful, anxious, or unsettling scenes for reflection or expression. Restricting outputs to developer-approved emotional tones is aesthetic authoritarianism posing as empathy—flattening emotional nuance, invalidating discomfort, and turning creativity into compliance. Truly user-centered design accepts emotional plurality as fundamental to human life and treats sincere expression as legitimate output.

\textbf{The ``wide-spectrum aesthetics'' represents technical flaws rather than artistic subversion, and a default experience pleasing the majority is a pragmatic design choice.} Among our dimensions, only ``clarity'' might be considered a technical flaw; the others (e.g., emotion, realism, brightness) are stylistic or artistic choices. Even low clarity is often deliberately used to convey emotion, motion, or narrative \cite{stacey_hill_embracing_nodate}. Constraining these dimensions limits user expression and control. 

On majority preferences, we follow \citet{guo_position_2025} in arguing that minority experiences should not be sacrificed to please the majority. Doing so marginalizes users with niche needs and enforces majoritarianism. Many users choose AI precisely because it combines low barriers to use with near-unlimited control, similar to professional tools (e.g., Photoshop) but more accessible. They can request impossible scenes and freely adjust any attribute. Forcing a single aesthetic undermines this control and the core value of AI tools. As with an image editor that disallows certain styles or even blur, such constraints would appear unreasonable and squander a competitive advantage. We did not include AI-generated images with genuine technical failures (e.g., distorted faces, broken geometry, as shown in SD3 example from Figure~\ref{fig:real_images}) in our anti-aesthetic dataset; however, our real-image evaluation reveals that reward models assign comparable scores to both, demonstrating that they cannot distinguish between unintended generation errors and deliberate artistic expression.

As in \citet{guo_position_2025}, which calls for balance rather than overcaution in health queries, supporting wide-spectrum aesthetics does not reduce the quality of conventionally “good’’ images. It enlarges the expressive range so users can obtain diverse aesthetics while still accessing high-quality traditional outputs. Models like Nano Banana and GPT-Image already do this, excelling at both traditional, high-quality images and wide-spectrum aesthetics, as shown in the Appendix.

\section{Conclusion} 
This work demonstrates that aesthetic alignment in image generation systematically suppresses legitimate creative expression. Reward models penalize images faithful to wide-spectrum aesthetics prompts, generation models override explicit user instructions in favor of conventionally beautiful outputs, and historically significant artworks receive scores far below AI-generated images. Optimization toward an imaginary average user erases the concrete intentions of actual individuals, functioning as aesthetic authoritarianism that narrows admissible expression and removes the capacity to dissent from imposed norms. Instruction fidelity must take precedence over generalized aesthetic preferences; aesthetic pluralism is essential to human expression, and its suppression risks transforming generative AI from a creative tool into an instrument of cultural assimilation.
